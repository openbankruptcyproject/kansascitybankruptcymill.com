What Is Chapter 7 Bankruptcy?

Chapter 7 is a liquidation bankruptcy. A court-appointed trustee reviews your assets, sells any non-exempt property to pay creditors, and remaining qualifying debts are discharged -- typically within 3 to 4 months of filing. In practice, most Chapter 7 cases are "no-asset" cases, meaning the debtor has no non-exempt property for the trustee to liquidate.

Chapter 7 eliminates most unsecured debts including:

  • Credit card balances
  • Medical bills
  • Personal loans
  • Past-due utility bills
  • Some older tax debts
  • Deficiency balances from repossessions or foreclosures

Chapter 7 does not discharge:

  • Student loans (except in rare hardship cases under 11 U.S.C. § 523(a)(8))
  • Recent tax debts (generally less than 3 years old)
  • Domestic support obligations (child support, alimony)
  • Debts from fraud or willful injury
  • Most government fines and penalties

Important: Unlike Chapter 13, where attorney fees can be paid through the plan, Chapter 7 fees are typically due before filing. Be wary of any attorney who asks you to charge the legal fees to a credit card -- this can create a nondischargeable debt if done shortly before filing.

Do You Qualify? The Means Test

To file Chapter 7, you must pass the means test under 11 U.S.C. § 707(b). The test has two parts:

Step 1: Income Comparison

Compare your household's current monthly income (averaged over the 6 months before filing) to the state median for your family size. If you're below median, you pass. Current median income thresholds for Kansas City area filers:

Step 2: Disposable Income Calculation

If your income exceeds the median, a detailed calculation subtracts allowed expenses (IRS standards plus actual secured debt payments) from your income. If your remaining disposable income is too low to fund a meaningful Chapter 13 plan, you still qualify for Chapter 7.

The Chapter 7 Timeline

1

Pre-filing credit counseling

Required within 180 days before filing. Approved agencies available online ($15-50). Takes about 1 hour.

2

Filing the petition

Your attorney files the petition, schedules, means test, and Statement of Financial Affairs. The automatic stay takes effect immediately. Filing fee: $338.

3

341 meeting of creditors (Day 21-50)

The 341 meeting is conducted by the Chapter 7 trustee. Typically lasts 5-15 minutes. You answer questions about your finances under oath. Creditors may attend but rarely do in consumer cases.

4

Objection deadline (Day 60 after 341)

Creditors and the trustee have 60 days after the 341 meeting to object to discharge or file a complaint under section 523. Most cases see no objections.

5

Post-filing debtor education

Second required course, completed after filing but before discharge. Online, $15-50, about 2 hours.

6

Discharge (Day ~90-120)

The court enters the discharge order. Qualifying debts are eliminated. 93%+ of Kansas City filers reach this step. The entire process typically takes 3 to 4 months.

Discharge Eligibility: The 727(a)(8) and 727(a)(9) Bars

Even if you pass the means test, you may be ineligible for a Chapter 7 discharge if you've filed bankruptcy before. Under 11 U.S.C. § 727(a)(8) and (a)(9):

  • Prior Chapter 7 discharge: Must wait 8 years before receiving another Chapter 7 discharge
  • Prior Chapter 13 discharge: Must wait 6 years (unless the prior Chapter 13 paid 100% of unsecured claims or 70% under a good-faith plan)

In W.D. Mo., 3.3% of Chapter 7 filers had a prior bankruptcy filing. If your attorney does not check these eligibility bars before filing, you could go through the entire process and be denied a discharge at the end.

Kansas and Missouri Exemptions

Exemptions determine what property you can keep in Chapter 7. Missouri and Kansas have different exemption systems:

Missouri Exemptions (Key Highlights)

  • Homestead: $15,000 ($30,000 for married couples filing jointly)
  • Vehicle: $3,000 per vehicle
  • Personal property: $3,000 aggregate
  • Wildcard: $600 plus up to $3,500 of unused homestead
  • Retirement accounts: Generally fully exempt (ERISA-qualified plans)

Kansas Exemptions (Key Highlights)

  • Homestead: Unlimited value, up to 1 acre in city / 160 acres rural
  • Vehicle: $20,000 (one vehicle)
  • Personal property: Various categories with specific limits
  • Retirement accounts: Generally fully exempt

Kansas has a stronger homestead exemption (unlimited value) and a significantly higher vehicle exemption ($20,000 vs. $3,000). If you live near the state line, which exemption system applies to you depends on where you have lived for the majority of the 730 days before filing. Consult an attorney about the domicile rules.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does Chapter 7 cost in Kansas City?
The court filing fee is $338. Attorney fees typically range from $1,000 to $2,500, paid before filing. Credit counseling courses cost $15-50 each (two required). Total typical cost: $1,400 to $2,900.
What is the Chapter 7 discharge rate in Kansas City?
In W.D. Mo., 93.1% of Chapter 7 cases filed in 2024 resulted in a discharge. The rate has stayed above 90% consistently since 2018. Less than 1% of cases are dismissed.
How long does Chapter 7 take?
Typically 3 to 4 months from filing to discharge. The 341 meeting is held 21-50 days after filing, and discharge enters about 60 days after that.
Will I lose my car?
In Missouri, you can exempt up to $3,000 per vehicle. In Kansas, the vehicle exemption is $20,000. If your car's equity (value minus loan balance) is within the exemption, you keep it. If you're making payments, you can typically keep the car by staying current or reaffirming the debt.
Will I lose my house?
If your home equity is within the exemption amount ($15,000 in Missouri, unlimited in Kansas) and you're current on mortgage payments, you can keep your home. If you're behind on payments, Chapter 13 may be a better option as it allows you to cure arrears through the plan.
Can I file Chapter 7 again after a previous bankruptcy?
You can file at any time, but discharge is barred for 8 years after a prior Chapter 7 discharge and 6 years after a Chapter 13 discharge (with limited exceptions). Use the free eligibility screener to check your dates.
Do I need an attorney for Chapter 7?
You can file pro se (without an attorney), but it is generally not recommended for most filers. Exemption planning, means test calculations, and reaffirmation decisions involve legal judgment. The 93%+ discharge rate assumes competent representation. See prosedebtors.org if you're considering self-representation.
How will Chapter 7 affect my credit?
A Chapter 7 filing stays on your credit report for 10 years. However, most filers see their credit scores begin recovering within 12-18 months after discharge, often reaching the mid-600s within 2-3 years. The discharge eliminates the debts that were damaging your score.
