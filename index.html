Market Concentration

The Kansas City Missouri-side bankruptcy market is one of the most concentrated in the country. A small number of attorneys handle the vast majority of cases - a structural condition that can enable mill-like practices when combined with poor outcomes.

What this means: 21 attorneys filing 200 or more Chapter 13 cases account for 91.1% of all filings in the Western District of Missouri. Whether a debtor gets adequate representation depends almost entirely on which of these attorneys they hire. And the dismissal rates across all tiers - 41% to 43% - suggest systemic problems that go beyond any single firm.

Why Concentration Matters

High market concentration is not inherently bad. An experienced attorney handling many cases can develop expertise and efficiency that benefits clients. The problem arises when volume outpaces capacity - when an attorney takes on more cases than they can adequately serve.

The distinguishing factor is outcomes. In the District of Kansas, high-volume attorneys (500+ cases) have a 26.2% dismissal rate. In the Western District of Missouri, that same category has a 42.0% dismissal rate - 16 percentage points higher. Same metro area, same economic conditions, same types of debtors. The gap is in the practice.

Why Cases Fail in Kansas City

When a Chapter 13 case is dismissed, the debtor loses their bankruptcy protection, may face resumed creditor collection activity, and has spent months or years making payments with nothing to show for it. In the Western District of Missouri, the primary causes of dismissal are:

Failed to pay filing fee13.0%
Failed to file required information8.4%

"Failed plan payments" is the most common reason, but this category masks a more nuanced reality. Plan failures can result from genuinely changed circumstances - job loss, medical emergencies, divorce. But they can also result from poorly designed plans that were never realistic, based on income and expense calculations that did not reflect the debtor's actual situation.

The "filed to fail" pattern: When an attorney files a Chapter 13 plan that is unlikely to succeed, the debtor still pays attorney fees (often through the plan itself), the attorney still earns their fee, and the client bears the full cost of failure. This creates a perverse incentive: the attorney profits regardless of outcome. Identifying this pattern requires comparing individual attorney dismissal rates against the court average.

Prior Filers

One in ten Chapter 13 filers in the Western District of Missouri (10.2%) had a prior bankruptcy filing in the same court. Prior filers face additional legal barriers under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f), which can bar them from receiving a discharge entirely. An attorney who files a case for a client who is legally ineligible for discharge is, at minimum, not performing adequate due diligence.

What To Look For

If you are considering hiring a bankruptcy attorney in the Kansas City area - or if you are already represented and wondering whether your case is being handled adequately - here are data-backed questions to ask:

1. What is your dismissal rate?

Any attorney should be able to tell you what percentage of their Chapter 13 cases are dismissed. If they don't know or won't say, that is itself a signal. The W.D. Mo. average is 44.4%. An attorney significantly above that deserves scrutiny.

2. How many active cases do you personally handle?

There is no magic number, but an attorney handling 200+ active Chapter 13 cases while also maintaining new case intake may not have the bandwidth for meaningful individual attention. Ask who will actually work on your case day-to-day.

3. Will you file my Monthly Operating Reports on time?

In Chapter 11 and some Chapter 13 cases, Monthly Operating Reports (MORs) are required. Chronic failure to file them is a red flag for attorney disengagement. Ask whether your attorney has any cases with delinquent MOR filings.

4. Have you checked whether I'm eligible for a discharge?

Under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f), prior filers face waiting periods before they can receive a discharge. This is a threshold question that should be answered before any filing. Use the free discharge eligibility screener to check yourself.

5. How are your fees structured?

In Chapter 13, attorney fees are typically paid through the bankruptcy plan - meaning you may not realize the full cost until years into your case. Ask for the total fee upfront, and compare it to the court-approved fee guidelines for your district.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a bankruptcy mill?
A bankruptcy mill is a high-volume law firm that processes large numbers of bankruptcy cases with minimal individual attention. These firms often have dismissal rates significantly above the court average, delegate most work to non-attorney staff, and profit regardless of whether the client's case succeeds. The term has no formal legal definition, but the pattern is well-documented in federal court data and recognized by judges, trustees, and bar associations.
What is the Chapter 13 dismissal rate in Kansas City?
In the Western District of Missouri (which covers the Missouri side of Kansas City), 44.4% of Chapter 13 cases filed between 2018 and 2024 were dismissed without the debtor receiving a discharge. The neighboring District of Kansas, covering the Kansas side of the metro area, has a dismissal rate of 35.5% - nearly 9 percentage points lower. Both courts serve the same metropolitan area.
How concentrated is the Kansas City bankruptcy market?
Extremely concentrated. Just 15 attorneys handle 80.6% of all Chapter 13 filings in the Western District of Missouri. These 500+ case attorneys have a 42% dismissal rate. By comparison, high-volume attorneys in the District of Kansas have a 26.2% dismissal rate - 16 percentage points lower.
Is a high-volume attorney automatically a bad choice?
No. Volume alone does not make a mill. Some high-volume attorneys achieve excellent outcomes through efficient systems and experienced support staff. The key metric is outcomes: what percentage of their cases result in a successful discharge versus dismissal. A high-volume attorney with a low dismissal rate may actually be a better choice than a low-volume attorney without the infrastructure to manage cases effectively.
How do I check my attorney's track record?
Public court records through PACER contain the outcome of every bankruptcy case. You can search by attorney name to see their full case history, including how many cases were dismissed versus discharged. The 1328f.com discharge screener also provides district-level data on outcomes. Additionally, your state bar association can tell you whether an attorney has any disciplinary history.
What should I do if I think my attorney is a mill?
If you believe your attorney is not providing adequate representation: (1) document specific failures - missed deadlines, unanswered communications, filing errors; (2) request a copy of your complete case file; (3) consult with another bankruptcy attorney for a second opinion; (4) file a complaint with your state bar association if warranted; (5) consider filing a motion to substitute counsel with the court. You have the right to competent representation under the Rules of Professional Conduct.
Can I file on the Kansas side instead of the Missouri side?
Bankruptcy venue is determined by where you have lived for the majority of the 180 days before filing. If you live in Kansas, your case will be filed in the District of Kansas. If you live in Missouri (including Kansas City, MO, Independence, Lee's Summit, etc.), your case will be filed in the Western District of Missouri. You cannot choose between districts based on preference.

About This Data

All statistics on this site are derived from the Federal Judicial Center Integrated Database, a public dataset maintained by the research arm of the federal judiciary. Case-level data is supplemented by public PACER docket records where noted.

This site does not identify individual attorneys by name. The purpose is to present aggregate data that helps Kansas City area residents make informed decisions about bankruptcy representation. Dismissal rates and volume tiers reflect patterns across the court as a whole.

This is an educational resource. It is not legal advice and should not be treated as a substitute for consultation with a qualified attorney.
