What Is Chapter 13 Bankruptcy?

Chapter 13 is a form of bankruptcy reorganization that allows individuals with regular income to repay some or all of their debts over a 3-to-5-year plan. Unlike Chapter 7, which liquidates non-exempt assets for a quick discharge, Chapter 13 requires monthly payments to a bankruptcy trustee who distributes the funds to creditors according to the plan.

Chapter 13 is often used by people who:

  • Earn too much to qualify for Chapter 7 under the means test
  • Want to save a home from foreclosure by curing mortgage arrears through the plan
  • Have non-exempt assets they want to keep
  • Need to repay priority debts like taxes or domestic support obligations
  • Had a prior bankruptcy that bars them from a Chapter 7 discharge under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f)

The appeal of Chapter 13 is real: it can stop foreclosure, halt wage garnishment, and provide a structured path to becoming debt-free. But the data shows that for many Kansas City filers, the promise of Chapter 13 does not match the outcome.

The Kansas City Chapter 13 Track Record

Between 2018 and 2024, 5,056 Chapter 13 cases were filed in the Western District of Missouri. Of the cases that have reached a final resolution:

44.4% were dismissed -- meaning the debtor lost bankruptcy protection, received no discharge, and may have spent months or years making payments for nothing. Only 48.7% resulted in a successful discharge.

The Plan Payment Problem

Failed plan payments account for 42% of all dismissals. But this number alone does not explain whether the failures are debtor-driven (genuine financial hardship) or attorney-driven (unrealistic plans filed without adequate budgeting).

When an attorney files a Chapter 13 plan with monthly payments the debtor cannot sustain, the case is set up to fail from day one. The debtor makes payments for months -- sometimes years -- before the plan is dismissed. The attorney, however, has already been paid (or is being paid through the plan). The incentive to ensure plan feasibility is structurally weak in high-volume practices where the attorney may never personally review the debtor's budget.

The Document Filing Problem

8.4% of dismissals occur because required documents were never filed. This is an attorney responsibility. Failure to file schedules, tax returns, or pay stubs is not a client problem -- it is a practice management problem. In a well-run office, these deadlines are calendared and tracked. In a mill, they fall through the cracks.

The Chapter 13 Process: What to Expect

If you are considering Chapter 13 in Kansas City, here is what the process looks like:

1

Pre-filing credit counseling

Required within 180 days before filing. Must be from an approved agency. Costs $15-50. Cannot be waived.

2

Filing the petition and plan

Your attorney files the petition, schedules, and proposed Chapter 13 plan with the court. The filing fee is $313. The automatic stay takes effect immediately, stopping most creditor collection activity.

3

The 341 meeting of creditors

Held 21-50 days after filing. The 341 meeting is conducted by the Chapter 13 trustee, who asks questions about your finances and plan. Creditors may attend but rarely do.

4

Plan confirmation

The court holds a confirmation hearing to approve your plan. Creditors may object. If confirmed, you make monthly payments to the trustee for 3-5 years.

5

Monthly payments (3-5 years)

You make payments to the trustee, who distributes funds to creditors. Below-median-income debtors: 3-year plan. Above-median-income: 5 years. Your attorney fees are typically paid through the plan.

6

Discharge (if successful)

After completing all plan payments and a post-filing debtor education course, the court enters a discharge order. Remaining qualifying debts are discharged. In W.D. Mo., only 48.7% of filers reach this step.

Discharge Eligibility: The 1328(f) Bar

Before filing Chapter 13, your attorney should check whether you are even eligible for a discharge. Under 11 U.S.C. § 1328(f), the court cannot grant a discharge if:

  • You received a Chapter 7 discharge within the last 4 years, or
  • You received a Chapter 13 discharge within the last 2 years

In the Western District of Missouri, 10.2% of Chapter 13 filers had a prior bankruptcy filing in the same court. Some of these filers may be legally ineligible for a discharge before their case is even filed. An attorney who does not check this before filing is not performing basic due diligence.

Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Chapter 13 Attorney

  1. What is your Chapter 13 dismissal rate? The W.D. Mo. average is 44.4%. Ask for their number. If they don't know it, that is a red flag.
  2. How many active Chapter 13 cases are you currently managing? An attorney with 300+ active cases may not have time for yours.
  3. Will you personally review my budget and plan? In many high-volume firms, paralegals prepare the plan with minimal attorney oversight.
  4. Have you checked my 1328(f) eligibility? If you have a prior bankruptcy, this must be verified before filing.
  5. What happens if my case is dismissed? Understand who keeps the fees and what options you have.
  6. What is your total fee, and how is it paid? Get the full number in writing. Compare to the district fee guidelines.
  7. How do you handle plan modifications? Life changes. Your attorney should be willing to modify the plan rather than let it fail.
  8. Can I reach you directly? Ask who will actually handle your questions. If the answer is "my paralegal," consider whether that is adequate for your situation.

When Chapter 13 Fails: Your Options

If you fall behind on plan payments or your financial circumstances change, your Chapter 13 case is at risk of dismissal. But dismissal is not automatic. You have several options, and understanding them before you need them can save your case.

Plan Modification

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 1329, you or your attorney can ask the court to modify your plan after confirmation. Common modifications include reducing the monthly payment amount, extending the plan duration (up to the 5-year maximum), or adjusting how creditors are paid. If your income has dropped due to job loss, medical issues, or other hardship, a modification may allow you to keep your case alive rather than losing everything you have paid so far. Your attorney should be proactive about proposing modifications when your circumstances change -- not waiting until the trustee files a motion to dismiss.

Hardship Discharge

In rare cases, you may qualify for a hardship discharge under 11 U.S.C. Section 1328(b), even if you have not completed all plan payments. To qualify, you must show that: (1) your failure to complete payments is due to circumstances beyond your control, (2) unsecured creditors have received at least as much as they would have received in a Chapter 7 liquidation, and (3) modification of the plan is not practicable. Hardship discharges are uncommon but can provide relief if you develop a serious disability, lose your primary income source permanently, or face other genuine hardship.

Conversion to Chapter 7

If your Chapter 13 plan is failing and you now qualify for Chapter 7, conversion may be your best option. Under 11 U.S.C. Section 1307(a), you have the right to convert your case to Chapter 7 at any time, provided the case was not previously converted from Chapter 7. Conversion gives you a fresh start with a Chapter 7 discharge in 3-4 months instead of continuing a plan you cannot complete. However, property that was protected by the Chapter 13 plan may be subject to liquidation in Chapter 7, so this decision requires careful analysis of your current exemptions.

Voluntary Dismissal

You can ask to dismiss your own Chapter 13 case. After dismissal, you lose the protection of the automatic stay, creditors can resume collection, and any mortgage arrears cured through the plan may become due again. If you refile Chapter 13 within one year, the automatic stay may be limited to 30 days under 11 U.S.C. Section 362(c)(3), or may not take effect at all if you had two or more prior cases dismissed in the past year.

The Cost of a Failed Chapter 13

When a Chapter 13 case is dismissed, the debtor does not just lose the bankruptcy protection -- they may lose years of payments with nothing to show for it. Understanding the true cost of failure is essential before deciding to file.

Attorney Fees in Dismissed Cases

In most Kansas City Chapter 13 cases, attorney fees are paid through the plan before unsecured creditors receive anything. This means your attorney may receive their full fee -- or a substantial portion of it -- even if your case is dismissed and you receive no discharge. The court approves these fees under 11 U.S.C. Section 330, and they are deducted from your plan payments. If your case fails after 2 years of payments, your attorney may have been paid $3,000-5,000 while you walk away with no debt relief.

Lost Time and Compounding Debt

A dismissed Chapter 13 case means 1-3 years of plan payments that did not result in a discharge. During that time, nondischargeable debts like student loans and recent taxes continued to accrue interest. If the case is dismissed, creditors who were not paid in full through the plan can resume collection for the full remaining balance -- plus any interest and fees that accumulated while the case was pending. For some debtors, their financial situation is worse after a failed Chapter 13 than it was before filing.

Impact on Future Filings

A dismissed case affects your ability to refile. If you refile within 180 days of dismissal, the automatic stay may be limited or eliminated entirely. Under Section 109(g), you may be barred from refiling for 180 days if the case was dismissed for willful failure to comply with court orders. Serial filings also draw increased scrutiny from the court and the U.S. Trustee.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Chapter 13 success rate in Kansas City?
In the Western District of Missouri, only 48.7% of resolved Chapter 13 cases result in a discharge. The District of Kansas performs better at 63.8%. A Chapter 13 "success" means completing all plan payments over 3-5 years and receiving a discharge of remaining qualifying debts.
How much does Chapter 13 cost in Kansas City?
Chapter 13 attorney fees in Kansas City typically range from $3,000 to $5,000, paid through the bankruptcy plan over 3-5 years. The court filing fee is $313. Total out-of-pocket depends on your plan terms. Importantly, attorney fees are often paid even when the case is dismissed -- meaning the attorney earns their fee regardless of whether you receive a discharge.
Why are so many Chapter 13 cases dismissed in Kansas City?
42% of dismissals in W.D. Mo. are due to failed plan payments. Other causes include failure to file required documents (8.4%) and failure to pay the filing fee (13%). The high dismissal rate may also reflect attorney practices: when 80.6% of cases are handled by just 15 attorneys, individual attention can suffer.
How long does Chapter 13 take in Kansas City?
A Chapter 13 plan runs 3 to 5 years. Below-median-income debtors may qualify for a 3-year plan; above-median-income debtors must commit to 5 years. If the case is dismissed before completion, the debtor receives no discharge and may have spent years making payments with no debt relief.
Can I switch from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7?
Yes, conversion from Chapter 13 to Chapter 7 is generally available under 11 U.S.C. § 1307(a) as a matter of right, provided the case was not previously converted from Chapter 7. This can be an important option if your circumstances change. However, Chapter 7 has its own eligibility requirements including the means test.
What happens to my house in Chapter 13?
Chapter 13 can stop foreclosure and allow you to cure mortgage arrears through the plan. As long as you make your plan payments and stay current on ongoing mortgage payments, you can keep your home. However, if the case is dismissed, the foreclosure protection ends and the lender can resume collection.
Should I file Chapter 13 or Chapter 7?
The choice depends on your income, assets, and goals. Chapter 7 is faster (3-4 months to discharge) and works well for people with limited income and few non-exempt assets. Chapter 13 is better if you need to save a home, repay priority debts, or don't qualify for Chapter 7. See our Chapter 7 guide for a detailed comparison.
